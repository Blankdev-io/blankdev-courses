Ethical persuasion

It is possible to use what we understand of behavioral psychology to help us persuade users to purchase without having negative consequences. We need to understand what our audience will find acceptable.

For example, most users will appreciate reviews and testimonials, as they help them make a decision. This is because they value the opinions of others—especially if they know the person.

Our tendency to rely on others in our decision making is often referred to as social proof, and is the reason why reviews and testimonials are seen in a positive light. Reviews help us reduce the risk of making a purchase for users, so they don’t feel manipulated. It also plays into the fact that avoiding risk is a deep seated psychological characteristic.

For example, users see an unconditional return policy as excellent customer service, not as an attempt to manipulate them into making a purchase.

Users will even still appreciate an unexpected gift from a company, despite the fact that this triggers our desire to reciprocate. Companies who give unexpected gifts to existing customers know that those customers are more likely to recommend them or buy again in future, but this is not normally seen as manipulative by users.

A good rule of thumb is to ask ourselves how a user would react if we openly and honestly explained what techniques we used to encourage them to buy online. If we suspect the user would be unhappy, then it is usually best to avoid that technique, because there will be a significant proportion of users who will see through any attempt to manipulate.

In many cases, a user will see your attempts to use psychology in design as a desire to better meet their needs and make them feel appreciated. This is ultimately about providing better customer service.

Psychology can also inform our design decisions and help us create better calls to action. In the next course in this series, we will take what we have learned about behavioral psychology and apply it to design and copy.
