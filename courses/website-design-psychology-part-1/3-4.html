Choice paralysis


Another factor to consider when reducing cognitive load is choice paralysis. The more choices we present a user, the longer they will take to make a decision.

It turns out there is a predictable relationship between the number of choices and the time it takes people to make a decision. That has become known as Hicks Law, named after William Edmund Hick who made the discovery.

The more choices we have, the more cognitive load we experience to make a decision. However, there is another factor to consider here. If it takes too long to make a decision, people tend to give up entirely. That is known as choice paralysis.

Ecommerce stores trying to provide too many products across too broad a category will face challenges in all kinds of areas, from fulfillment to marketing. Most significantly, they will overwhelm users and reduce their conversion rate. It is more advisable to instead find a niche.

However, this is not to say all choices are terrible. Where there is a clear difference between those choices, the cognitive load is reduced. Picking between 24 varieties of jam is challenging, but choosing between 24 wide-ranging food types would not be as hard because the choices are distinct.

The same is true with navigation. Even three choices of navigational items can be overwhelming if there is no apparent difference between each.
