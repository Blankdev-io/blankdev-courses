The cost of over-thinking


The reason cognitive load is so critical is that it directly relates to our inherent need to conserve energy, an important survival trait.

The more demanding a conscious mental process, such as complex multiplication, the more effort is required. That is not only true mentally; it also has an impact physically. For example, when doing a stressful mental task, our heart rate increases and pupils dilate.

This is why we favor subconscious thinking. In other words, we prefer recalling facts such as the capital of France. This kind of thinking puts far less strain on us.

However, there is another reason to consider cognitive load: if users are required to over-think, they routinely miss essential information.

A prominent example of this is the Invisible Gorilla Experiment carried out by Daniel Simons and Christopher Chabris. In this experiment, participants were asked to watch a video in which two teams were passing basketballs back and forth. They were asked to count the number of times one of the teams passed the ball, a task that required a great deal of concentration.

While carrying out the task, over 50 percent of participants completely missed the fact that a person dressed as a gorilla walked prominently into the shot and beat its chest before walking off. Their cognitive load was so high that they missed valuable additional information.

The fact that cognitive load can cause us to miss crucial information, and that we inherently avoid having to think too hard, makes the usability of our interfaces crucially important.

How then can an understanding of psychology help us address this issue? Three principles can help:

  • Mental models
  • Choice paralysis
  • Perception of ease
