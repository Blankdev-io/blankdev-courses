Unfacilitated usability testing

Like both in-person and remote usability testing, unfacilitated testing typically requires a user to complete one or more tasks. However, unlike its counterparts, users are not observed in real time. Instead, users are typically given instructions about what tasks they should complete, and their sessions are recorded.

Users are also encouraged to explain their actions and thinking as they go, which helps to understand the motivations of the user’s actions.

The lack of a facilitator can be challenging. It does not allow for the questioning of participants, or to help course correct if users misunderstand the task or instructions. However, it also has two advantages over facilitator-led testing:

  • It allows for the testing of a large number of participants because sessions don’t require the researcher and participant to be available at the same time.
  • Services such as User Testing can be used to quickly carry out ad-hoc tests. User Testing will recruit users and have results back, often in under an hour.

Because unfacilitated testing requires little effort, it’s ideal for regular use throughout the development process. However, because a facilitator is not there to support users, it’s hard to test anything in an early phase of development when the prototype is incomplete or unstable. This limitation makes unfacilitated testing better suited to later stages in development.
